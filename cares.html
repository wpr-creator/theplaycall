The $1,200 Check

Every qualifying adult in America got the same amount. But $1,200 does not feel the same everywhere it lands.

The Setup

The Economy Shut Down Overnight

In March 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic forced governments across the world to shut down businesses, schools, and public spaces to slow the spread of the virus. In the United States, GDP fell by 32 percent in a single quarter — the sharpest economic drop in recorded history.

Restaurants, salons, gyms, retail stores, and entertainment venues closed. Millions of Americans lost their jobs within weeks. Unemployment reached 14.7 percent in April 2020 — the highest rate since the Great Depression. Families who had been living paycheck to paycheck had no paycheck at all.

Congress moved faster than it had in decades. On March 27, 2020 — less than two weeks after the shutdown began — President Trump signed the CARES Act into law. At $2.2 trillion, it was the largest economic relief package in American history.

The Intervention

Same Check — Different Weight

The CARES Act did more than send checks. It expanded unemployment benefits by $600 per week on top of state payments. It created the Paycheck Protection Program — PPP — which gave forgivable loans to small businesses that kept their employees on the payroll. It provided $500 billion in loans for large corporations and $150 billion for state and local governments.

But the $1,200 checks are what most people remember — because most people got one. And research conducted after the payments arrived showed something important about how government spending actually works in the real world.

Rent. Groceries. Utilities. Bus fare. The money moved immediately into the local economy and kept circulating.

Most of it went into savings or investments. The money largely stopped moving after the first transaction.

This is the multiplier effect in action — and its limits. The government injected the same dollar into every household. But the dollar generated far more economic activity when it landed where people spend immediately. The design of the policy — a flat payment to everyone — was politically simple but economically imprecise.

The Seen and The Unseen

What the CARES Act Did — and Did Not — Fix

What Was Visible

The economy did not collapse the way it did in 2008. Unemployment fell rapidly after the initial shock — from 14.7 percent in April 2020 to under 7 percent by December. Consumer spending recovered faster than most economists predicted. Many small businesses survived that would otherwise have closed permanently.

The stimulus checks provided genuine relief to tens of millions of families who were living paycheck to paycheck and had nowhere else to turn.

What Was Harder to See

The CARES Act added $1.7 trillion to the national debt over the following decade. Inflation followed — by 2022, prices for groceries, gas, and rent had risen sharply, which hit low-income households the hardest because they spend a higher share of their income on necessities.

Undocumented immigrants — many of them essential workers who kept grocery stores and hospitals running during the pandemic — received nothing. No check. No expanded unemployment. No PPP loans.

Term Bank

Key Terms for This Event

Expansionary Fiscal Policy
Government action to stimulate a contracting economy by spending or cutting taxes. The CARES Act is the largest single example of expansionary fiscal policy in U.S. history.
Multiplier Effect
One dollar of government spending generates more economic activity than the original amount. The CARES Act showed the multiplier works faster when money lands where people spend immediately.
National Debt
The total accumulated amount the government owes. The CARES Act added $1.7 trillion to the national debt — a cost future taxpayers will carry.
Regressive Tax
A tax that takes a higher share of income from lower earners. The inflation that followed the CARES Act functioned like a regressive tax — rising prices hit low-income households hardest.
